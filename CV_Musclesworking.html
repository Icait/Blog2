Calcium and Contraction

Muscle cells are made up of myofibrils, with thin and thick filaments. The thick filament is myosin and the thin filament is actin. Ca2+ ions bind to a globular molecule, troponin, which pulls the alpha helical tropomyosin down. This exposes the binding site on actin for myosin heads. The ATP attached to the myosin head is hydrolysed with the catalystic element mg. This releases adp and Pi. The myosin flexes pulling the actin filament closer. This forms the famed cross-bridges. That's why when muscles contract, they are said to be shorter and wider. This cycle is repeated, with ATP binding to the myosin head, removing it from the actin. Ca2+ ions are pumped back into the SR through terminal pumps or channels.

Sliding Filament Theory

The sliding filament theory is the most widely accepted explanation for how this occurs. According to this theory, muscle contraction is a cycle of molecular events in which thick myosin filaments repeatedly attach to and pull on thin actin filaments, so they slide over one another. The actin filaments are attached to Z discs, each of which marks the end of a sarcomere. The sliding of the filaments pulls the Z discs of a sarcomere closer together, thus shortening the sarcomere. As this occurs, the muscle contracts.
